**Spearheading Electoral Peace: Systematic Literature Review on the Role of ICT in Promoting Transparency and Trust in Elections**
#
Digital misinformation poses growing threats to electoral integrity, public trust, and democratic peace. In developing democracies, elections now demand not only physical safeguards but also strong digital resilience. This systematic literature review examines how Information and Communication Technologies (ICT) enhance electoral transparency and promote positive peace. Guided by Johan Galtung’s peace formula, the study synthesizes findings from studies published between 2020 and 2025. Using the PICO framework, the review evaluates democratic populations (P), ICT-enabled interventions (I), comparisons to non-digital methods (C), and outcomes (O) related to equity (E), harmony (H), trust (T), and conflict (C). Findings reveal ICT’s dual role in election processes. Tools like Nigeria’s BVAS and Kenya’s KIEMS improve voter verification and reduce fraud, increasing equity and institutional harmony. In contrast, unregulated systems, deepfakes, and Internet shutdowns worsen trauma and conflict, especially where digital literacy and infrastructure are limited. The study highlights that ICT’s peacebuilding potential depends on transparent implementation, ethical design, and strong governance. This review reconceptualizes electoral ICT not merely as an administrative instrument, but as a conditional peacebuilding mechanism whose impact depends on governance design, institutional trust, and digital literacy capacity. Technology alone cannot solve electoral challenges, but responsible ICT use can help safeguard electoral integrity and strengthen democratic resilience in vulnerable political environments.
